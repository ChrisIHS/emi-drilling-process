Phase 1Pre-Spud / Office
Step 1.1 Scope & Engagement Operator hires engineering firm. Scope defined — single well, multi-well program, or retainer. Fee structure agreed (supervisor day rate + engineering hours).
Step 1.2 Well Design Casing design, bit program, directional plan, mud program, completions design. Every design decision drives an AFE cost estimate.
⚡EMI: Historical cost data from past wells populates AFE line items with actual rates — not memory or old spreadsheets.
Step 1.3 AFE Preparation & Approval All cost estimates assembled into the Authority for Expenditure. Submitted to operator for management/board approval before any work begins. The AFE is the financial contract the engineering firm is held to.
Step 1.4 Service Company Selection & Contract Negotiation Engineering firm recommends and selects: drilling contractor, directional company, mud company, cement contractor, rental tools, wireline. Negotiates day rates and pricing on operator's behalf.
⚡EMI: Structured historical rate data gives engineering firm documented basis for negotiations — actual vs. quoted rates from prior wells.
Step 1.5 Regulatory Submissions Well licence applications, H₂S EPZs, surface location approvals, environmental assessments. All required before spud authorization.
Step 1.6 Drilling Program Preparation Detailed drilling programs written for each well phase — surface hole, intermediate, curve, lateral. Distributed to drilling contractor and all service companies before rig spud.
Step 1.7 Rig & Service Company Mobilization Engineering firm coordinates rig move scheduling, service company mobilization timing, and pre-spud wellsite preparation. Company man assigned and deployed to location.
Phase 2Active Drilling / Field
Step 2.1 Wellsite Supervision (24hr Coverage) Engineering firm company man on location — single supervisor on day shift or two rotating 12-hr shifts on complex wells. Every wellsite decision flows through them.
Step 2.2 DDR Review & Rig Status Approval Company man reviews drilling contractor's Daily Drilling Report each morning. Verifies every hourly status code — drilling, tripping, waiting, NPT. Approves or disputes. On a $25K/day rig, a disputed 4-hr status code is a $4,000 decision.
⚡EMI: DDR captured digitally. Status codes locked in real time. Company man approves on mobile — dispute-proof timestamp on every decision.
Step 2.3 Third-Party Field Ticket Review & Signature Every service company (mud, directional, cement, wireline, rentals, fuel, water, waste) generates a ticket for their work. Company man reviews each one and signs as operator's authorized representative. 8–15 tickets/day on a busy well.
⚡EMI: Service reps submit tickets digitally. Company man reviews and digitally signs in seconds — timestamped, geolocation-tagged, immediately transmitted. No paper, no lag.
Step 2.4 Daily Cost Compilation & AFE Update Company man compiles all approved costs, assigns each to the correct AFE line item, calculates actual vs. budget. Done in a spreadsheet each evening — currently takes 45–90 minutes per day.
⚡EMI: Costs auto-coded to AFE lines the moment tickets are approved. Dashboard updates in real time. Zero manual compilation. Company man gets that 60–90 minutes back every day.
Step 2.5 Daily Reporting to Operator Compiled cost summary plus drilling progress update sent to operator's drilling engineer and finance team — typically by email before 8am. Primary window into well performance.
⚡EMI: Operator accesses live dashboard at any time. No morning report required. Operator sees costs updating in real time throughout the day.
Step 2.6 Real-Time Engineering Decisions Formation tops, bit performance, mud weight adjustments, casing setting depths, directional corrections. Each technical decision may have cost implications requiring AFE flag and operator notification.
Step 2.7 Service Company Coordination Engineering firm coordinates arrival/departure timing for all service companies — cementing crews, wireline runs, perforating, testing. Ensures right people and equipment are on location at the right time to avoid standby charges.
Step 2.8 AFE Variance Management When costs trend over a line item — extra mud chemicals, additional cement job, fishing tools after twist-off — engineering firm identifies early, communicates to operator, determines if AFE supplement required. Late identification = costly delays.
⚡EMI: Automatic variance alerts fire when a cost category approaches its AFE limit — before it becomes a surprise. Operator can authorize a supplement while the well is still drilling.
Phase 3Invoice Management (Concurrent)
Step 3.1 Field Ticket Collection & Transmission Signed paper tickets from every service company must travel from the rig to the operator's office — scanned, photographed, or physically mailed. Creates a constant administrative loop between wellsite and back office.
⚡EMI: Approved digital tickets are instantly available to the operator's AP team the moment the company man signs. Zero transmission delay. Nothing lost in transit.
Step 3.2 Invoice Review & Approval Routing Service companies convert field tickets to invoices and submit to operator's AP portal. Engineering firm reviews each invoice against the original field ticket — confirming quantities, rates, and AFE coding are correct — before routing for payment.
⚡EMI: Approved ticket auto-converts to correctly formatted invoice. Rates and coding pre-populated from the approved ticket. Engineering firm reviews exceptions only, not every line.
Step 3.3 Multi-Portal Management Different operator clients use different AP portals — OpenInvoice, SAP Ariba, Coupa, Jobutrax, GEP Smart. Engineering firm managing multiple operator programs logs into multiple portals in different formats with different workflows. Significant administrative overhead.
⚡EMI: Single submission platform handles formatting and routing to whichever portal each operator uses. One workflow regardless of how many operator clients or portals.
Step 3.4 Dispute Resolution When a service company disputes a rate or quantity — or operator AP flags an invoice — engineering firm is the intermediary. Pulls original field ticket, verifies what was approved, resolves discrepancy. Paper-based tickets make this a slow, manual investigation.
⚡EMI: Digital timestamp and real-time sign-off create an indisputable contemporaneous record. Most disputes eliminated at source. Those that arise resolve in minutes, not weeks.
Step 3.5 Service Company Payment Tracking Engineering firm tracks which service company invoices have been paid and which remain outstanding — not because they cut the cheques, but because unpaid service companies call the company man, not the operator's AP team. Managing that communication is part of the service.
⚡EMI: Approval-to-submission pipeline visible in real time. Engineering firm can see exact status of every invoice — submitted, approved, pending, paid — without phone calls.
Step 3.6 Engineering Firm's Own Fee Submission Magus-style firms charge the operator for their own services — field supervisor day rates, engineering hours, project management fees. These are tracked and submitted separately from third-party service costs, and must also be coded to the correct AFE lines.
⚡EMI: Engineering firm's own time and fees tracked in the same platform — submitted against the AFE alongside all third-party costs for a complete program cost picture.
Phase 4Well Completion / Post-TD
Step 4.1 Final Cost Compilation & AFE Reconciliation All costs compiled into final well cost summary — every ticket, every invoice, every AFE line — reconciled against the original budget. On paper-based systems with 300–500 tickets across 30–90 days, this takes 5–10 business days.
⚡EMI: Automated reconciliation report generated at well TD. Every ticket matched to every invoice matched to every AFE line. Ready in minutes. Complete audit trail included.
Step 4.2 AFE Supplement Documentation Any costs exceeding AFE lines documented with justification — what happened, why it was necessary, who authorized it. Part of the operator's permanent capital accounting record and any future regulatory or audit requirement.
Step 4.3 Well File Compilation Complete well file assembled — all DDRs, field tickets, invoices, AFE reconciliation, mud reports, cement records, directional surveys, HSE records — and delivered to operator as their permanent well record.
⚡EMI: All digital records automatically organized and exportable as a complete well file. No manual assembly. Fully audit-ready from day one.
Step 4.4 Post-Well Performance Review Engineering firm reviews what went over budget, under budget, which service companies performed well, and what they would do differently. This data should improve the next AFE estimate and the next contract negotiation.
⚡EMI: Structured performance data from every well — cost per metre, NPT rate, AFE accuracy by line, vendor rate vs. actual — automatically compiled. Every well makes the next bid sharper.
Step 4.5 Operator Cost Report Delivery Final well cost report delivered to operator — actual vs. AFE by line item with variance explanations. This is the primary evidence of the engineering firm's cost management performance and the foundation for contract renewal.
⚡EMI: Report generated automatically from the digital record. Professionally formatted, complete, and delivered at well TD — not weeks later. Demonstrates cost management discipline to the operator.
Step 4.6 Service Company Rate Leverage for Next Well Engineering firms that ran clean, fast approval cycles on this well have earned goodwill with service companies. Faster payment cycle means service companies prefer their programs — and may offer preferred pricing or priority mobilization on the next job.
⚡EMI: Engineering firm can document payment cycle performance — average days from ticket approval to payment — as evidence in the next contract negotiation. "Our programs pay in 35 days. The market average is 60."
Step 4.7 Lessons Learned → Next AFE Post-well data feeds directly back into the pre-spud process for the next well — closing the continuous improvement loop. Better AFE estimates, better vendor negotiations, better program design.
⚡EMI: Historical cost database grows with every well. Engineering firm builds a proprietary cost intelligence asset that compounds in value over time — improving every future estimate, negotiation, and bid.
